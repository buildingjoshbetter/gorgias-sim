The idea

Democracy's real failure mode is not a rigged vote. It is a legislature passing a law that nobody in the room actually understood the consequences of. The vote is downstream. The damage is written into the statute.

Statecraft Simulator is a flight simulator for lawmaking. You type a policy change in plain language — "raise the residence homestead exemption to $140,000" — and it pushes that draft through a Vulcan-scale corpus before it ever reaches a committee. Which statutes does it amend. Which existing rules does it now contradict. Which agencies are compelled to issue implementing regulations. Which budget lines move, in which direction, by how much. Who bears the burden, mapped county by county. And what it opens the state up to in court.

This is Justinian run forward in time. Their agents answer "what does the law say" against records that already exist. We answer "what would this law do" against records that don't exist yet — by simulating the amendment against the corpus and letting the conflicts, mandates, and fiscal shocks surface on their own. Simulation before legislation. That is the upgrade to democracy nobody at this hackathon is building.

How it works

A plain-language draft is compiled into an amendment, applied against the corpus as a diff, and the fallout is scored on four axes — conflicts, mandates, fiscal, and litigation — then rendered as one cascade.

Every node in the cascade carries its citation — the exact statute section, the reg authority, the budget strategy line. Grounded before generated: the simulation is a diff over primary sources, not a guess about them.

Real vs staged

Real: the cascade renderer, wave animation, conflict flares, county heat-map, fiscal counter, litigation column — all live and interactive from structured data.

Staged: the amendment is pre-compiled and its fallout pre-resolved from real sources rather than diffed live over 157B records. The scoring engines are real; we don't traverse the full corpus on stage.

Honest framing to judges: "the cascade is real; the diff that produces it is exactly what Justinian does against your corpus at scale."

Pros / cons

Pros

  • Fuses three Vulcan verticals at once — fiscal notes, statutory interpretation, red-tape — into one artifact.
  • The thesis lands hardest of any concept: simulate before you legislate. Deeper than any voting app in the room.
  • The wow is cinematic and self-explaining: the wave, the gold conflict flares, the map filling in, the counter climbing.
  • Directly leverages Tribunal and Reflected — engines no one else can credibly claim to have built.
  • Every node is citation-backed. Grounded before generated, audit trail native.

Cons

  • Most ambitious build in the set — four-tier cascade plus a heat-map is a lot of SVG in three hours.
  • One draft only; the "any bill, any state" promise is asserted, not shown live.
  • Fiscal figures are illustrative — directionally defensible, not audited to the appropriation.
  • Real corpus diffing over 137 corpus types is genuinely hard; we stage it, and must frame that honestly.
Vulcan alignment

This is the concept that fuses Vulcan's verticals instead of picking one. It reads fiscal notes (#5), statutory interpretation (#6), and red-tape reduction (#12) as a single motion.

Justinian serves government staff drafting and analyzing policy. Statecraft Simulator is what a legislative counsel or a budget office should see the instant a draft exists: the conflicts, the mandates, the fiscal shock, the litigation exposure — before markup, not after passage.

Trajan is citizen-facing. The same cascade, simplified, answers a constituent's "what would this bill do to my county" with a map and a number instead of a press release. Grounded in the same records their agents already run on.
